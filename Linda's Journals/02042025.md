02/04/2025

Weather: Sunny

Mood: ███████

I skipped two days, not because nothing happened, but because everything stayed the same in a way that made it harder to face, like watching something slowly sink beneath the surface and realizing you can’t tell when it finally disappears. There’s a kind of quiet that comes with that, a stillness that doesn’t comfort, only lingers, and writing about it felt like admitting that I couldn’t change anything. But today is different, in a way I can’t ignore, in a way that sits too heavily to leave unwritten. The Contractor has always had that quirk, that dense, unmoving shadow resting over her eye, like a piece of darkness that chose to stay, and today… Star has it too. The same thick shadow now covers her eyes, hiding the bright golden iris that used to define her, that soft light that once felt like warmth even from a distance, something steady, something reassuring. Now it’s gone from view, and I can’t tell if it’s still there underneath or if it’s already fading into something I can’t reach.

The Contractor said Star completed a task, and by her own philosophy, she gives what is earned without question, without hesitation, without asking why it was done. So whatever Star did, whatever she chose to go through alone, this shadow is the result of it, something given, something taken, something that now stays with her whether she wants it or not. I kept asking her why, holding onto the question like it might anchor her back to me, like if she answered even once, I could understand enough to help, but she didn’t say anything at all. Not a word, not even a hint, just that same silence that has been growing around her, thicker than the frost she carries. And eventually, everyone else stopped trying. They stopped approaching her, stopped comforting her, stopped believing there was something they could do, and now they just leave her alone, like distance is safer than care, like getting too close might only make things worse.

Lucy AI wrote new protocols overnight, lines of instruction meant to prevent escalation, to keep people safe if they encounter her, to manage something that used to never need managing. Two people didn’t follow them, maybe out of habit, maybe out of disbelief, maybe because they remembered who she used to be and thought that was still enough. It wasn’t. They’re gone now, left behind as ice statues, silent and unmoving, like warnings carved into the space around her. And her frost… it isn’t the same anymore. It’s no longer something that settles and fades, no longer something temporary. It’s permafrost now, something deeper, something that holds on and refuses to let go, something that feels like it belongs to a place where warmth doesn’t return. Doors freeze shut when she’s near, sealed by the cold she doesn’t seem to notice, and every time I come to see her, she has to open them herself, like even the facility is beginning to resist her presence, like the world around her is slowly deciding it can’t keep up.

Her clothes have changed too, in quiet ways that say more than they should. They used to be clean and white, untouched, almost gentle in how simple they were, but now they’re tinted azure, stiff and hardened by the frost that never leaves her, like even the things closest to her have started to reflect what she’s becoming. And still… her garden is alive. Somehow, impossibly, it continues to grow, to exist in a space that should have killed it. I think the plants adapted, learned to survive in the cold instead of resisting it, bending instead of breaking, finding a way to keep going in conditions that don’t forgive. I think I did the same, in my own way, because I’m still here too, still standing beside her even when I don’t understand what I’m standing in.

But something else has changed, something quieter, something that hurts more the longer I think about it. She doesn’t visit Stitch as much anymore. Not like before, not with that same urgency, not with that same need to stay by his side for hours without moving. And I don’t know why. Maybe she already knows the outcome in a way I can’t accept, maybe she’s seen something I haven’t, or maybe… she’s starting to let go, slowly, carefully, the same way she pulled away from everything else. I keep telling her he’s fine, every time I see her, repeating it like it might become true in a way she can believe, telling her that Charlie repaired his arm and leg, that they’re stable, that he’s still here and that he’ll wake up. Charlie is one of the best, someone people trust without hesitation, someone who knows what she’s doing even when things seem impossible, and Star should trust her. I even broke the rules for this, took something I wasn’t allowed to take, stole Stitch’s analysis just to show her, just to give her something real, something solid, something she couldn’t dismiss as empty reassurance. It took time to convince her to even look at it, to even consider that maybe things weren’t as hopeless as they felt, but eventually she did.

And still… Star is still there, but not in the way she used to be. Not fully, not brightly, not in a way that fills the space around her. The light she carried, the one that made everything feel softer, easier, something people leaned toward without realizing it, is fading, little by little, like it’s slipping away without asking, like it doesn’t need permission to disappear. And all I can do now is hope for one thing, something small and fragile that feels like it’s all that’s left to hold onto—that she never stops clinging, not to Stitch, not to us, not to whatever part of herself is still reaching out even now. That she never lets go completely, because if she does, I don’t know what will be left of her, or of any of this.

I’m starting to get concerned. This isn’t normal.
