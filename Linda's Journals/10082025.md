# 10/08/2025

10/08/2025  
 Mood: Surprised  
 Weather: Sunny

Star was… cheerful today.

Not in the way she used to be, not fully, not without weight behind it—but enough to feel real, enough to catch me off guard. She’s still in her Betrayal form, the white dress still lingering like a memory that refuses to fade, but today… she smiled. A small one, fragile, like something relearned rather than remembered, but it was hers. And that alone felt like something I hadn’t seen in far too long.

Her recovery has been accelerating since the surgery. Not just physically, though that part is undeniable—she moves better, steadier, her presence no longer flickering like it might disappear at any moment—but something deeper too. She can speak now, in short bursts, words that come carefully, like she’s testing them before letting them exist. It’s not much, but it’s enough to remind me that she’s still here, still trying.

And today… it felt like she was getting better.

When I entered her room, I found her sitting quietly, a tray of cupcakes in front of her. She was eating them slowly, almost absentmindedly, that same soft smile resting on her face like she had found something simple to hold onto again. It was such a small, ordinary moment—but after everything, it felt almost unreal.

The first time in weeks…

she looked happy.

I wanted to leave it at that. I really did. To accept the moment, to let it stay untouched, to not question something that finally looked like peace.

But something felt wrong.

There was a faint green residue on the tray. Barely noticeable, something most people would overlook without a second thought. But I’ve been looking too closely for too long now. I’ve learned that the smallest details are the ones that matter most.

So when she wasn’t looking…

I took a sample.

I told myself it was nothing. That I was just being paranoid. After everything, I didn’t know how to stop expecting something to go wrong.

But the results didn’t care about what I hoped.

It was the same substance.

The same anti-regeneration residue used in her attack.

The same thing that nearly killed her.

And it was on the cupcakes.

Rose-tinted cupcakes.

And only one person makes those.

Rose.

Star’s friend. One of her closest. The one she trusted without hesitation, without doubt. The one who treated her like family long before I was ever part of this.

It didn’t make sense.

It couldn’t.

Rose would never do something like this. Not to her. Not to Star.

But the evidence was there, and it didn’t leave room for denial.

So we brought her in.

For interrogation.

Rose panicked the moment she realized what was happening. Not anger, not defensiveness—just fear. Raw, immediate, uncontrollable fear. The kind that doesn’t look like guilt, but doesn’t clear it either. She didn’t say anything at first. Not a single word.

Star was there too.

Standing across from her.

And the look in her eyes…

it wasn’t just anger. It wasn’t just pain. It was something deeper, something quieter, something that didn’t need to be spoken to be understood.

*Why did you do this to me?*

Rose couldn’t answer.

But then—

Star moved.

She pointed, slowly, deliberately, not at her face, not at her hands, but at her neck.

Rose froze.

And then… she nodded.

Like she understood exactly what Star meant.

And in that moment, everything shifted.

I told Director Lucious to jam all signals. Every connection, every possible line in or out—cut it completely. If something was controlling her, watching her, waiting for her to fail… it needed to be silenced first.

We waited.

The seconds stretched, heavier than they should have been.

Then confirmation came.

Nothing was getting through.

And only then…

Rose spoke.

Her voice trembled, uneven, like every word had to fight its way out. On a routine patrol in ██████████, she and her brother were captured. Taken by Urbanshade, without warning, without a chance to resist. They didn’t ask for information. They didn’t negotiate.

They gave her an order.

Kill Star.

Break the morale of the entire facility.

And if she failed—

the device implanted in her neck would activate.

A failsafe.

A punishment.

An execution.

Not just for her.

For her brother too.

Five days.

That’s all they gave her.

Five days to choose between the person she loved like a sister…

and the only family she had left.

And now…

there was only one day left.

The room fell into silence.

And I looked at Star.

The hatred in her eyes didn’t disappear all at once. It didn’t vanish like it was never there. It softened, slowly, like something was being understood instead of rejected.

And then—

her Betrayal form began to crack.

Not physically, not violently, but like the idea itself could no longer hold together. Like the meaning behind it was unraveling under something stronger, something quieter, something that didn’t need to force itself to exist.

The white dress faded.

The knife disappeared.

The shattered halo… was gone.

And when it ended—

she stood there, changed.

Her wings were back.

Both of them.

Whole.

Radiant.

They lit the room in a way I can’t fully describe, something warm, something alive, something that didn’t hurt to look at anymore. It wasn’t overwhelming—it was gentle, steady, like a light that chose to stay instead of burn.

I knew what it was.

“Forgiveness.”

Not weakness.

Not forgetting.

Something stronger than both.

And then—

she moved.

Her hand came down against the table, firm, certain, the sound cutting clean through everything that had been said, everything that had been revealed.

And she spoke.

Clear.

Steady.

Certain.

“Let’s make a plan.”

And just like that—

this stopped being a story about survival.

And became something else entirely.
