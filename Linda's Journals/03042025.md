# 03/04/2025

03/04/2025

Weather: █████████

Mood: Calm

Star is very different today, in a way that feels too complete to be temporary, too deliberate to be accidental, like something has finally settled into its final shape. Her usual Frost form is gone, not faded, not suppressed, but replaced entirely by something far more defined, something that doesn’t feel like an emotion anymore, but a state of being. She wears a magnificent azure dress, beautiful in a way that almost hurts to look at, something that doesn’t seem crafted or stitched but conjured, as if it came into existence the moment she accepted whatever she’s becoming. An ice wand rests along her back like a wizard’s tool, natural on her as if it has always belonged there, while the bandages around her remain unchanged, a quiet reminder that beneath all of this, something is still broken.

Her eyes are still covered by that same heavy shadow, the one I already wrote about, the one that hides whatever light might still remain, and with every step she takes, faint trails of frost linger behind her, soft and fleeting yet undeniable, like the world itself is marking where she’s been. A crown rests on her head, tightened by vines that curl and bind instead of decorate, something regal but restrained, something that feels less like honor and more like inevitability. But the most noticeable thing, the thing I can’t stop looking at no matter how hard I try, is the roses. Azure-tinted white roses cover her body, wrapping around her arms, her legs, her torso, blooming in the cold like they belong there, like they grew from her rather than being placed upon her.

She told me this is the true form of her Frost.

The Queen of Frost.

And hearing that… it felt like something inside me dropped, because this is what I was afraid of, not the sadness, not the silence, but this—this quiet escalation into something deeper, something heavier, something that doesn’t pass. This isn’t just sadness anymore. It’s grief. The kind that stays, the kind that reshapes everything around it until there’s nothing left untouched.

She keeps muttering the same thing, over and over, like a thought that won’t leave her, like something she’s trying to convince herself of. “Stop clinging onto things that left you.” And every time she says it, I try to answer, I try to reassure her, to tell her that not everything is gone, that not everything leaves, that some things are still here, still holding on with her. I say it again and again, even when I’m not sure if she hears me the way she used to.

Star is still there. I can feel it, in the way she looks at me sometimes, in the way she hasn’t completely turned away. But something has changed in a way that’s harder to ignore now. She doesn’t always let me in anymore. The door that used to open for me without question now stays closed sometimes, and I’m left standing outside, waiting, hoping, wondering what’s happening on the other side that I’m no longer allowed to see.

The cold around her has gotten worse, sharper, more absolute. Water freezes the moment it comes near her, instantly, without hesitation, like the space around her has forgotten what warmth is supposed to feel like. Even with my frost-proof jacket, I can feel it now, seeping through, settling into my bones, making me shiver in a way I didn’t before. And it’s not just the cold itself—it’s what it represents, how far things have gone, how much stronger it’s become.

Star is distancing herself.

Not all at once, not in a way that can be easily stopped, but steadily, like everything else she’s done.

And I can’t stop thinking about one thing, something that won’t leave my mind no matter how much I try to push it away—that maybe Stitch’s coma isn’t what created this version of her. Maybe it was only the beginning, only the first crack in something much deeper, something far more devastating that’s been there all along, waiting, growing, until it finally became something even she couldn’t hold back anymore.
